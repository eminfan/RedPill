# 4 Group Formation: Memetic Domestication and Identity Construction

## 4.7 Memetic Domestication Mechanism

### Core Proposition

**The system converts discrete individual energy into group entropy reduction (order) through memetic domestication.**

Memetic domestication is not violent coercion, but through rule design, semantic implantation, and cognitive packaging, makes individuals actively orient behavior toward group interests. This is the core mechanism for social systems to maintain order:

### Essence of Memes

- **Definition**: Memes are the basic unit of culture, ideas, behaviors, symbols etc. transmitted through imitation
- **Function**: Memes are the "operating system" of social systems, responsible for rule, institution, and narrative construction
- **Characteristics**: Memes have self-replication, variation, and selection properties, similar to genes

### Goals of Memetic Domestication

1. **Energy Conversion**: Convert individual scattered energy into group ordered energy
2. **Behavior Coordination**: Make individual behavior conform to group interests
3. **Order Maintenance**: Achieve social stability through cognitive unification

### Three Core Mechanisms of Memetic Domestication

#### 1. Selective Redundancy (Redundancy Shielding Law)

**Definition**: The system deliberately sets unnecessary complex rules that exceed the cost needed to solve problems, whose real function is not to solve problems, but to screen compliant people.

**Operating Logic**:
- The more complex the rules, the more individual energy is consumed
- Complex rules require large learning costs
- The higher the learning cost, the less likely individuals are to question the system
- Independent thinkers will be eliminated by complex rules
- Compliant people will pass rule screening and enter the system

**Typical Cases**:
- Ancient imperial examination: Eight-legged essay and other extremely formatted requirements screened out civil servants who absolutely obeyed central government orders
- Modern workplace: Lengthy reimbursement processes or meetings, screening out employees who can tolerate 996 and obey management
- Legal system: Complex legal provisions, making ordinary people difficult to understand and question

**Counter Methods**:
- Calculate the "screening cost" of redundancy
- Choose "low-cost compatibility"—obey core rules, ignore redundant processes
- Protect individual energy, rather than passively being trained

#### 2. Semantic Anchoring (Semantic Anchoring Law)

**Definition**: The system locks individual cognition within a certain framework through specific vocabulary, symbols, or narratives, making it impossible to see the system's real structure.

**Operating Logic**:
- Semantic anchors reduce individual thinking costs
- Anchors become the "default framework" of cognition
- Any thinking deviating from anchors is seen as "abnormal"
- Anchors unify group cognition, reduce system entropy

**Typical Cases**:
- Political terms: "Freedom," "democracy," "justice" etc. are given specific meanings
- Economic terms: Standardized definitions of "success," "efficiency," "competition" etc.
- Cultural symbols: National flags, national anthems, festivals etc. that enhance group identity

**Counter Methods**:
- Semantic de-anchoring: Identify the real function of vocabulary
- Multi-dimensional thinking: Examine the same concept from different perspectives
- Redefine: Express real feelings in your own language

#### 3. False Subjectivity (Entropy-Energy Conversion Law)

**Definition**: Packaging group energy-consuming behaviors as "self-realization" or "autonomous choice," reducing resistance to energy conversion.

**Operating Logic**:
- The system packages group-needed behaviors as individual "free choice"
- Through ideological propaganda, makes individuals think behavior is out of their own will
- Individuals in the illusion of "autonomous choice," actively contribute energy to the system
- The system gains stable energy input, while individuals gain psychological satisfaction

**Typical Cases**:
- Consumerism: Packaging purchasing behavior as "personality expression" and "self-realization"
- Workplace culture: Packaging overtime as "struggle spirit" and "career development"
- Political participation: Packaging voting as "democratic right" and "civic responsibility"

**Counter Methods**:
- Energy tracing: Monitor your energy flow direction
- Motive analysis: Distinguish real needs from system-instilled needs
- Autonomous decision: Make choices based on real needs

## 5.2 Identity Construction Mechanism

### Core Proposition

**Group identity is the product of memetic domestication, the psychological foundation for individuals to integrate into groups.**

### Levels of Identity

1. **Personal Identity**: Individual's cognition of their own identity
2. **Group Identity**: Individual's sense of belonging to the group they belong to
3. **Cultural Identity**: Individual's identification with specific culture

### Steps of Identity Construction

1. **Classification**: Divide individuals into different groups
2. **Labeling**: Give groups specific symbols and characteristics
3. **Contrast**: Emphasize differences inside and outside the group
4. **Reinforcement**: Strengthen group identity through rituals and narratives

### Functions of Identity

1. **Cohesion Function**: Enhance internal cohesion of the group
2. **Norm Function**: Guide individual behavior to conform to group norms
3. **Protection Function**: Provide psychological safety net for individuals
4. **Mobilization Function**: Inspire individuals to act for group interests

## 4.9 Evolutionary Logic of Groups

### Core Proposition

**The formation and evolution of groups is a strategy choice of gene groups to maximize their own fitness.**

### Types of Groups

1. **Blood Groups**: Groups based on blood relations (such as families, clans)
2. **Territorial Groups**: Groups based on geographic space (such as communities, nations)
3. **Cultural Groups**: Groups based on cultural identity (such as ethnic groups, religions)
4. **Interest Groups**: Groups based on common interests (such as enterprises, political parties)

### Evolutionary Mechanisms of Groups

1. **Group Formation**: Individuals form groups for survival and development
2. **Group Competition**: Resource competition between different groups
3. **Group Selection**: Characteristics beneficial to group survival are retained
4. **Group Evolution**: Group structure and function evolve with environmental changes

### Boundaries of Groups

- **Physical Boundaries**: Entity boundaries such as geography, organizations
- **Cultural Boundaries**: Cultural boundaries such as language, values
- **Psychological Boundaries**: Psychological boundaries such as identity, sense of belonging
