# Case Analysis: Complete Lifecycle of Soviet Planned Economy System

## Case Overview

**Soviet Planned Economy System** (1917-1991) was one of the most important socio-economic experiments of the 20th century. From its birth in the October Revolution of 1917 to its demise with the Soviet Union's dissolution in 1991, spanning 74 years, it fully experienced the complete process of "desire convergence → behavior convergence → institutional solidification → structure emergence → institutional rigidity → institutional demise."

Unlike cases of gradual abolition and institutional reconstruction, the Soviet planned economy is a typical case of "system collapse, system restart."

---

## I. Desire Convergence (1917-1921)

### 1.1 Background: Discrete Individual Desires

Before the October Revolution of 1917, Russia's social structure was highly differentiated:

| Group | Core Desire | Discrete State |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| Workers | Survival desire: Higher wages, better conditions | Scattered across factories, lacking organization |
| Peasants | Survival desire: Obtain land | Each fighting alone, village community traditions |
| Soldiers | Survival desire: End war, go home | War-weariness widespread |
| Intellectuals | Development desire: Modernization, reform | Diverse thoughts, no unified direction |
| Bourgeoisie | Reproduction desire: Protect property, maintain status | Fear revolution, preparing to flee |

**Problem**: Social contradictions were acute, but various groups' desires were scattered, unable to form unified power.

### 1.2 Convergence Effect of Memetic Domestication

**Convergence Mechanism 1: Semantic Anchoring**

Bolsheviks redefined "success":

| Old Definition | New Definition |
|----------------|----------------|
| Individual struggle | "Bourgeois ideology" |
| Private property | "Exploitation tool" |
| Market exchange | "Capitalist remnants" |
| Collectivism | "Proletarian consciousness" |

**Convergence Mechanism 2: Selective Redundancy**

- During civil war, opponents were eliminated or exiled
- Binary opposition of "White Army" vs "Red Army"
- Non-compliant were purged

**Convergence Mechanism 3: False Subjectivity**

Revolutionary slogans made masses "feel" they were pursuing their own goals:

> "All power to the Soviets"
> "Land to peasants, factories to workers"
> "Peace, land, bread"

**Result**: Dispersed individual desires were converged to the unified direction of "proletarian revolution."

### 1.3 Environmental Pressure Accelerates Convergence

| Pressure Source | Effect |
|-----------------|--------|
| War pressure | WWI defeat, social collapse |
| Economic pressure | Inflation, material shortage |
| Political pressure | Tsarist autocracy lost legitimacy |
| International pressure | Foreign intervention, siege isolation |

**Convergence Threshold Breakthrough**: October 1917, Bolsheviks seized power, began constructing planned economy.

### 1.4 War Communism: Extreme Form of Convergence

1918-1921, Soviet Russia implemented "War Communism":

- Grain requisitioning: Forced collection of peasant grain
- Industrial nationalization: All factories nationalized
- Ban on private trade: Markets eliminated
> Labor obligation: Forced labor

**This is the extreme form of desire convergence**—through coercive means, all individual desires were converged to the goal of "war victory."

## II. Behavior Convergence (1921-1928)

### 2.1 New Economic Policy: Temporary Relaxation

1921, War Communism led to economic collapse, Lenin implemented New Economic Policy (NEP):

- Allowed private trade
- Allowed small private enterprises
- Grain tax replaced grain requisitioning

**This is temporary interruption of behavior convergence**—institution forced to relax, individual behavior re-diversified.

### 2.2 Stalin Model: Re-Convergence

1928, Stalin began "Five-Year Plans," behavior convergence re-strengthened:

| Behavior Area | Convergence Manifestation |
|---------------|---------------------------|
| Labor mode | State enterprises, collective farms |
| Distribution mode | Wage grade system, rationing system |
| Lifestyle | Unified housing, unified education |
| Thought expression | Official ideology, propaganda slogans |

**Example**:

> **Collectivization: Coercive Form of Behavior Convergence**
> 
> 1929-1933, Soviet Union forcibly promoted agricultural collectivization:
> - Eliminated kulak class
> - Forced peasants to join collective farms
> - Eliminated private land
> 
> Degree of behavior convergence:
> - Where to work: Collective farm
> - How to work: Unified arrangement
> - What to get: Unified distribution
> 
> This is coercive form of behavior convergence—non-conformers eliminated.

### 2.3 Driving Forces of Convergence

**Driving Force 1: Meme Transmission**

- Propaganda machine full coverage
> "Stakhanovite movement" set examples
- Literature, film, art unified themes

**Driving Force 2: Group Pressure**

- "Enemy of the people" label
- Great Purge terror
- Neighbors, colleagues' surveillance

**Driving Force 3: Interest Binding**

- Wages linked to plan completion rate
- Promotion linked to political performance
- Housing, education, healthcare bound to work unit

## III. Institutional Solidification (1928-1953)

### 3.1 Trigger Conditions for Institutionalization

| Condition | Soviet Situation |
|-----------|------------------|
| Behavioral pattern stability | State enterprises, collective farms became normal |
| Scale expansion | Covered entire Soviet Union |
| Frequent conflicts | Class struggle needed regulation |
| Intergenerational transmission | Need to pass institution to next generation |

### 3.2 Mechanisms of Institutionalization

**First Stage: Written Text**

- 1936 Constitution: Established socialist system
- Planning law: Regulated economic activities
- Labor code: Regulated labor relations

**Second Stage: Legitimacy Narrative**

| Narrative Content | Function |
|-------------------|----------|
| "Socialist superiority" | Prove institutional rationality |
| "Catch up and surpass capitalism" | Grant sacred mission |
| "Proletarian dictatorship" | Legitimize one-party rule |
| "Planned economy = science" | Package political choice as science |

**Third Stage: Forced Execution**

- State Security Committee (KGB): Surveillance of society
- Gulag: Punish dissidents
- Household registration system: Restrict population movement

### 3.3 Institutional Functions

**Function 1: Rapid Industrialization**

- 1928-1937, two five-year plans
- Industrial output grew 4 times
> From agricultural country to industrial country

**Function 2: Social Security**

- Free education
- Free healthcare
- Full employment (nominally)

**Function 3: Military Power**

- WWII victory
- Nuclear weapons
- Superpower status

**Key Insight**: Planned economy indeed created huge achievements in early period, this was an important source of its legitimacy.

## IV. Structure Emergence (1930s-1960s)

### 4.1 Emergence of Three-Layer Structure

According to social trophic level theory, Soviet planned economy emerged a three-layer structure:

| Level | Role | Function | Benefits |
|-------|------|----------|----------|
| Enrichment layer | Party-state high-level, privileged class | Make plans, allocate resources | Villas, special supply stores, privileged healthcare |
| Transmission layer | Middle-level cadres, enterprise managers | Execute plans, transmit resources | Higher income, position privileges |
| Production layer | Workers, peasants | Create value, produce products | Wages, rations |

**Resource Flow Direction**:

```
Production layer (workers peasants labor)
    ↓ Create surplus
Transmission layer (cadres management)
    ↓ Intercept portion
Enrichment layer (party-state high-level)
    ↓ Extract surplus
```

### 4.2 Parasitic Coefficient Analysis

**Parasitic Coefficient (Pc)**: Proportion of resources occupied by privileged class without contributing

| Period | Parasitic Coefficient | Manifestation |
|--------|----------------------|---------------|
| 1930s | Higher | Great Purge, frequent cadre turnover |
| 1950s-1960s | Medium | Khrushchev reforms, limited privileges |
| 1970s-1980s | Extremely high | Brezhnev era, privileges solidified |

**Example**:

> **Privileged Class: Embodiment of Parasitic Coefficient**
> 
> Soviet privileged class enjoyed multiple privileges:
> - Special supply stores: Imported goods, scarce materials
> - Special healthcare: Specialized hospitals, sanatoriums
> - Special education: Children enter elite schools
> - Special housing: Villas, high-end apartments
> 
> These privileges didn't come from labor contribution, but from institutional status.

### 4.3 Formation of New Class

Yugoslav scholar Djilas proposed "New Class" theory:

- Bureaucratic class became new ruling class
- They didn't own means of production, but controlled means of production
- They exercised power in the name of "the people"

**This is important insight of structure emergence**: Planned economy intended to eliminate classes, but created new classes.

## V. Institutional Rigidity (1964-1985)

### 5.1 Manifestations of Rigidity

| Manifestation | Specific Situation |
|---------------|-------------------|
| Form-substance disconnect | Plan completion rate falsification, data inflation |
| Beneficiary group solidification | "Gerontocracy," cadre lifetime tenure |
| Adaptability decline | Cannot cope with technological revolution, market changes |
| Innovation vitality loss | Stifle reform attempts, suppress dissent |

**Example**:

> **Data Falsification: Form-Substance Disconnect**
> 
> Brezhnev era:
> - Factories inflated output to complete plans
> - Farms inflated harvests to get rewards
> - Localities inflated achievements to please superiors
> 
> Plans formally "completed," actually production efficiency low.

### 5.2 Reasons for Rigidity

**Reason 1: Interest Lock-in of Institutional Beneficiaries**

- Bureaucratic class benefited from institution
- They blocked any reform attempts
- 1965 Kosygin reform dissolved by bureaucratic system

**Reason 2: Path Dependence**

- Already invested huge resources
- Political cost of changing institution extremely high
- "Socialism" had become political symbol

**Reason 3: Backfire Effect of Meme Legitimization**

- Questioning planned economy = Questioning socialism
- Opposing Soviet model = Opposing Marxism
- Any reform faced ideological resistance

**Reason 4: Missing Information Feedback Mechanism**

- No market price signals
- No competitive pressure
- No effective supervision

### 5.3 Consequences of Rigidity

| Indicator | Data |
|-----------|------|
| Economic growth rate | 1960s: 5%, 1970s: 3%, 1980s: 1% |
| Technology gap | Widened from the West |
| Living standards | Stagnant, daily necessities shortage |
| Corruption level | Increasingly serious |

**Institution Became the Problem Itself**: Planned economy intended to improve efficiency, but became an obstacle to development.

## VI. Institutional Crisis (1985-1991)

### 6.1 Legitimacy Crisis

| Legitimacy Source | Collapse Manifestation |
|-------------------|------------------------|
| Performance legitimacy | Economic stagnation, living standards decline |
| Ideological legitimacy | Official ideology lost persuasiveness |
| International legitimacy | Cold War disadvantage, lost superpower status |

**Trigger Event**: 1985 Gorbachev came to power, began "Perestroika and New Thinking."

### 6.2 Functional Crisis

- Institution couldn't provide basic consumer goods
- Technology backward, couldn't compete
- Fiscal crisis, debt accumulation

**Example**:

> **Shortage Economy: Embodiment of Functional Crisis**
> 
> Late 1980s Soviet Union:
> - Queuing for shopping became daily life
> - Basic daily necessities shortage
> - Consumer goods quality poor
> 
> Planned economy completely lost its core function of "meeting people's needs."

### 6.3 Execution Crisis

- Central directives couldn't be executed
- Localities went their own way
- Bureaucrats passively resisted reform

**Example**:

> **Bureaucratic Resistance: Embodiment of Execution Crisis**
> 
> Gorbachev reforms:
> - Top level proposed reform plans
> - Middle-level bureaucrats passively executed
> - Reform effects greatly discounted
> 
> Institution could no longer execute effectively.

## VII. Institutional Demise (1991)

### 7.1 Trigger of Demise

**August 1991, conservative coup failed**:

- Coups tried to restore old order
- Coup failure accelerated institutional collapse
> Union republics declared independence one after another

**This is the critical point of institutional demise**—coup failure marked the end of old institution.

### 7.2 Process of Demise

```
1985: Gorbachev began reforms
    ↓
1988-1989: Eastern European revolutions
    ↓
1990: Union republics declared sovereignty
    ↓
August 1991: Coup failed
    ↓
December 1991: Soviet Union officially dissolved
```

### 7.3 Method of Demise

**System Collapse**:

- Not gradual abolition
- Not peaceful transition
- But dramatic collapse

**Characteristics**:

| Characteristic | Explanation |
|----------------|-------------|
| Suddenness | Complete collapse within months |
| Comprehensiveness | Political, economic, social simultaneous collapse |
| Violence level | Relatively low (compared to French Revolution) |

### 7.4 Post-Demise Reconstruction

**System Restart**: Russia turned to market economy

| Old Institution (Planned Economy) | New Institution (Market Economy) |
|-----------------------------------|----------------------------------|
| State ownership | Private ownership |
| Planned distribution | Market regulation |
| Fixed prices | Free prices |
| One-party rule | Multi-party system |

**Shock Therapy**:

- Price liberalization
> State enterprise privatization
- Trade liberalization

**Consequences**:

| Indicator | Change |
|-----------|--------|
| GDP | Declined 40% from 1990-1996 |
| Inflation | Reached 2500% in 1992 |
| Poverty rate | Rose from near zero to 40% |
| Life expectancy | Male declined 5 years |

**Key Insight**: The cost of system restart is extremely high.

## VIII. Theoretical Mapping and Summary

### 8.1 Complete Lifecycle Mapping

| Theory Stage | Soviet Planned Economy Case | Time |
|--------------|----------------------------|------|
| Desire convergence | Revolutionary slogans converged masses' desires | 1917-1921 |
| Behavior convergence | Collectivization, industrialization forced convergence | 1921-1928 |
| Institutional solidification | Constitution, planning law established institution | 1928-1953 |
| Structure emergence | Three-layer structure, privileged class formed | 1930s-1960s |
| Institutional rigidity | Data falsification, low efficiency | 1964-1985 |
| Institutional crisis | Economic stagnation, legitimacy collapse | 1985-1991 |
| Institutional demise | Soviet dissolution, system collapse | 1991 |

### 8.2 Key Variable Analysis

| Variable | Impact |
|----------|--------|
| Initial conditions | Backward agricultural country, high industrialization pressure |
| External environment | Cold War confrontation, arms race |
| Information feedback | Missing, leading to error accumulation |
| Reform timing | Too late, couldn't save |
| Path dependence | 74 years accumulation, hard to change |

### 8.3 Theoretical Verification

This case verifies the following propositions of transition theory:

1. **Desire Convergence Theorem**: Revolution can converge masses' desires through memetic domestication
2. **Behavior Convergence Theorem**: Coercive means can accelerate behavior convergence
3. **Institutional Solidification Theorem**: Legitimacy narrative can solidify institutions
4. **Structure Emergence Theorem**: Planned economy inevitably emerges privileged class
5. **Institutional Rigidity Theorem**: Interest lock-in and information missing lead to rigidity
6. **Institutional Demise Theorem**: System collapse is one way of institutional demise

## IX. Comparative Analysis of Institutional Demise Methods

### 9.1 Comparative Analysis

| Dimension | Gradual Reform Cases | Soviet Planned Economy |
|-----------|----------------------|------------------------|
| Duration | Longer (e.g., British enclosure ~400 years) | 74 years (1917-1991) |
| Origin method | Evolution + Design | Revolution + Design |
| Rigidity reason | Interest lock-in, class conflict | Interest lock-in, information missing |
| Demise method | Gradual reform | System collapse |
| Reconstruction path | Institutional reconstruction | System restart |
| Social cost | Lower | Extremely high |

### 9.2 Determinants of Demise Method

| Factor | Gradual Reform Cases | System Collapse (Soviet) |
|--------|----------------------|--------------------------|
| Reform timing | Earlier, institution not completely rigid | Too late, institution completely rigid |
| Reform leadership | Top-down active reform | Passive response to crisis |
| Alternative solutions | Have mature alternatives | Lack mature solutions |
| Social consensus | Society generally supports reform | Society divided, lacks consensus |
| International environment | Open, integrated into world | Cold War confrontation, isolated closed |

### 9.3 Key Insights

| Insight | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| Reform timing is crucial | Earlier reform, lower cost |
| Gradual better than radical | Gradual reform reduces social shock |
| Alternative solutions must be prepared | Restart without solutions is disaster |
| Information feedback mechanism indispensable | Systems without feedback inevitably rigidify |
