# Afterword: Planting Trees on Ruins

---

## I. Cold and Heat

After finishing this theory, I often ask myself late at night:

**What can these cold words actually give a person?**

They can't pay your mortgage, can't find you a job, can't stop a war, can't bring a dead person back. They are just a way of explaining—about how the world turns.

But explanations have their own power.

When you see how money flows from your pocket into someone else's vault, you might reconsider that "opportunity."
When you see who stands behind the slogans that make your blood race, you might think one more second before charging forward.
When you see who set the standards keeping you awake with anxiety, you might exhale—and get a good night's sleep.

**Words are cold. But waking up is hot.**

Not the heat of blood boiling—the heat of cold water splashed on your face in winter, and your whole body coming alive.

---

## II. The Price

But I must be honest: **waking up costs something.**

You will be angry. When you see the calculations behind things that have "always been this way," you'll want to smash something. After you smash it, the things are still there, but you are no longer the same person.

You will feel emptiness. When you discover that even the thought "I want" might have been planted in your head by someone else, you'll ask: Then what is actually mine? What's the point of trying? What does choice even mean?

You will be lonely. Everyone around you seems to be doing fine—working, paying loans, scrolling phones, sleeping. They don't ask why, and they don't need to. But you asked, and you can never go back. You can't pretend you didn't see, just like you can't pretend you never smelled something burning.

These are the costs.

Lu Xun said: **"A true warrior dares to face the bleak reality of life, dares to look the dripping blood squarely in the eye."**

I am no warrior. But I know that **the pain of being awake beats the sweetness of being numb**. Because being awake gives you at least one thing—you know what you're choosing. Even if your choice turns out to be exactly the same as before, this time, it's yours.

---

## III. And Then What

Many people ask me: **So you see it now, and then what?**

And then what? Nothing. There's no next page of the secret manual, no hidden door past the final level.

But I can tell you what changed for me—

I developed an instinctive wariness toward information. When I see a news story, an advertisement, a "everyone thinks so" opinion, I pause: Who's speaking? What do they want me to believe? What's in it for them? This isn't about rejecting everything—it's about not letting everything reject my right to think.

I began examining my own desires. When I want something, I ask: Is this me wanting it, or did someone make me want it? This isn't about killing desire—it's about standing up straight in front of it, instead of kneeling.

I stopped blaming everything on individuals. A person is poor—not necessarily because they're lazy. A person is grinding—not necessarily because they're greedy. Often, the shape of the cage determines the posture of the person. Seeing the structure isn't about excusing individuals—it's about finding the place where you can actually do something.

None of this is earth-shattering. It hasn't changed the world. But it changed mine.

---

## IV. Please Dismantle It

I don't expect you to accept all of this wholesale. Quite the opposite—

**Question it.** Find the holes, point out the absurdities, tell me where I'm wrong.
**Test it.** Weigh it against what you've seen with your own eyes.
**Surpass it.** If one day you find a better way of explaining, throw this away without hesitation.

The fate of any explanation should be to be questioned, tested, and surpassed. If it's only accepted, memorized, and enshrined—then it becomes a new cage. And a cage is precisely what it set out to saw through.

---

## V. The Saw and the Tree

In the preface, I said this theory is a saw.

Now I need to add something: **The saw is not the destination.**

After you saw through the cage, what's outside? A wasteland. No signposts, no streetlights, no one to plan your direction for you. The wind is fierce, and you can't see far.

Some will be afraid, turn back, decide the cage was at least warm.
Some will stand in place, holding the saw, not knowing which way to step.
Some will walk a few paces, find nothing on the wasteland, and sit back down.

But someone will crouch down on the wasteland, break open the cracked earth, and bury a seed.

No one guarantees the seed will sprout. Planting trees on a wasteland is, by nature, a thing without guarantees.

But this is the most "alive" thing I can imagine, after seeing everything for what it is—

**Not pretending not to see. Not being paralyzed by anger. But planting trees on ruins.**

Romain Rolland said: **"There is only one heroism in the world: to see the world as it is, and to love it."**

Knowing the cage is a cage, and still choosing to walk out.
Knowing the wasteland is a wasteland, and still choosing to plant.
Knowing the seed might not sprout, and still choosing to bend down.

---

## VI. Finally

Thank you for reading this far.

Whatever you end up believing or doubting, I hope these words leave you with something—even if it's just one question, one pause, one thought that makes you sit up suddenly in the middle of the night.

May you, after seeing the cage, still have the courage to walk out.
May you, after seeing the wasteland, still have the patience to plant.
May you, after seeing human nature, still have tenderness for people.
