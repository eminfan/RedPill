# 4 Group Formation: Meme Domestication and Identity Construction

## 4.7 Meme Domestication Mechanism

### Core Proposition

**The system transforms discrete individual energy into group entropy decrease (order) through meme domestication.**

Meme domestication is not violent coercion, but through rule design, semantic implantation, and cognitive packaging, making individuals actively orient their behavior toward group interests. This is the core mechanism by which social systems maintain order:

### The Essence of Memes

- **Definition**: Memes are the basic units of culture—ideas, behaviors, symbols, etc. transmitted through imitation
- **Function**: Memes are the "operating system" of social systems, responsible for constructing rules, institutions, and narratives
- **Characteristics**: Memes possess self-replication, mutation, and selection properties, similar to genes

### Goals of Meme Domestication

1. **Energy Conversion**: Transform individuals' dispersed energy into group's ordered energy
2. **Behavioral Coordination**: Make individual behavior conform to group interests
3. **Order Maintenance**: Achieve social stability through cognitive unification

### Three Core Mechanisms of Meme Domestication

#### 1. Selective Redundancy (Redundancy Shielding Law)

**Definition**: The system deliberately sets unnecessarily complex rules that exceed the cost needed to solve problems; their true function is not solving problems but screening for compliant individuals.

**Operational Logic**:
- The more complex the rules, the more individual energy is consumed
- Complex rules require large learning costs
- The higher the learning costs, the less likely individuals are to question the system
- Independent thinkers are eliminated by complex rules
- Compliant individuals pass through rule screening and enter the system

**Typical Cases**:
- Ancient imperial examinations: Eight-legged essay and other extremely formatted requirements screened out civil officials who absolutely obeyed central government decrees
- Modern workplaces: Lengthy reimbursement processes or meetings screen out employees who can tolerate 996 and obey management
- Legal systems: Complex legal provisions make it difficult for ordinary citizens to understand and question

**Counter-Methods**:
- Calculate the "screening cost" of redundancy
- Choose "low-cost compatibility"—comply with core rules, ignore redundant processes
- Protect individual energy rather than passively undergo training

#### 2. Semantic Anchoring (Semantic Anchoring Law)

**Definition**: The system uses specific vocabulary, symbols, or narratives to lock individual cognition within a certain framework, making it impossible to see the system's true structure.

**Operational Logic**:
- Semantic anchor points reduce individual thinking costs
- Anchor points become the "default framework" for cognition
- Any thinking that deviates from anchor points is viewed as "abnormal"
- Anchor points unify group cognition, reducing system entropy

**Typical Cases**:
- Political terminology: Words like "freedom," "democracy," "justice" are given specific meanings
- Economic terminology: Standardized definitions of concepts like "success," "efficiency," "competition"
- Cultural symbols: Flags, national anthems, holidays and other symbols that enhance group identity

**Counter-Methods**:
- Semantic de-anchoring: Identify the true function of vocabulary
- Multi-dimensional thinking: Examine the same concept from different perspectives
- Redefinition: Express genuine feelings in your own language

#### 3. False Subjectivity (Entropy-Energy Conversion Law)

**Definition**: Packaging group energy-consuming behaviors as "self-actualization" or "autonomous choice," reducing resistance to energy conversion.

**Operational Logic**:
- The system packages group-needed behaviors as individuals' "free choice"
- Through ideological propaganda, making individuals believe their behavior is based on their own will
- In the illusion of "autonomous choice," individuals actively contribute energy to the system
- The system obtains stable energy input while individuals gain psychological satisfaction

**Typical Cases**:
- Consumerism: Packaging purchasing behavior as "personal expression" and "self-actualization"
- Workplace culture: Packaging overtime as "striving spirit" and "career development"
- Political participation: Packaging voting as "democratic rights" and "civic responsibility"

**Counter-Methods**:
- Energy tracking: Monitor your own energy flow direction
- Motive analysis: Distinguish genuine needs from system-instilled needs
- Autonomous decision-making: Make choices based on genuine needs

## 5.2 Identity Construction Mechanism

### Core Proposition

**Group identity is a product of meme domestication and the psychological foundation for individuals integrating into groups.**

### Levels of Identity

1. **Personal Identity**: Individual's cognition of their own identity
2. **Group Identity**: Individual's sense of belonging to their group
3. **Cultural Identity**: Individual's identification with a particular culture

### Steps in Identity Construction

1. **Classification**: Dividing individuals into different groups
2. **Marking**: Conferring specific symbols and characteristics upon groups
3. **Contrast**: Emphasizing differences between inside and outside the group
4. **Reinforcement**: Strengthening group identity through rituals and narratives

### Functions of Identity

1. **Cohesion Function**: Enhancing internal cohesion within the group
2. **Normative Function**: Guiding individual behavior to conform to group norms
3. **Protective Function**: Providing individuals with a psychological safety net
4. **Mobilization Function**: Stimulating individuals to act for group interests

## 4.9 Evolutionary Logic of Groups

### Core Proposition

**The formation and evolution of groups are strategic choices made by gene groups to maximize their own fitness.**

### Types of Groups

1. **Kinship Groups**: Groups based on blood relations (e.g., families, clans)
2. **Territorial Groups**: Groups based on geographic space (e.g., communities, nations)
3. **Cultural Groups**: Groups based on cultural identity (e.g., ethnic groups, religions)
4. **Interest Groups**: Groups based on common interests (e.g., enterprises, political parties)

### Evolutionary Mechanisms of Groups

1. **Group Formation**: Individuals form groups for survival and development
2. **Group Competition**: Resource competition between different groups
3. **Group Selection**: Characteristics beneficial to group survival are retained
4. **Group Evolution**: Group structure and function evolve with environmental changes

### Boundaries of Groups

- **Physical Boundaries**: Geographic, organizational, and other tangible boundaries
- **Cultural Boundaries**: Language, values, and other cultural boundaries
- **Psychological Boundaries**: Identity, sense of belonging, and other psychological boundaries
