# Independent Thinking: From Shell-Riding to Autonomy

## Core Proposition

**Independent thinking is not "all ideas are self-originated," but "being able to identify what has been implanted, then consciously choosing to accept or reject."**

From the perspective of meme group evolution theory, true independent thinking is a scarce ability—because what the vast majority of people consider "independent thinking" is essentially shell-riding computation by meme groups.

### 1.1 Pre-Installation of the Cognitive Operating System

From birth, meme groups pre-install a unified "cognitive operating system" in each individual through channels such as family, education, media, and social interaction:

| Pre-Installed Content | Source | What You Think |

| Emotional rules | Media, social interaction | "These are my genuine feelings" |

**This system was installed before you had the capacity for reflection; you've never questioned its source.**

### 1.2 The Concealment of Shell-Riding Thinking

Shell-riding thinking is difficult to detect because it disguises itself as "my own thoughts":

```
Meme implantation → Internalized as "my idea" → Thought to be independent thinking → Continues propagating memes
```

**Key Trap**: You won't suspect "your own thoughts," because that is "you."

### 1.3 Reinforcement of Group Consensus

When you and everyone else are saying the same things and expressing the same emotions, you become even more convinced "this is right":

- Everyone thinks "success means having money"

**Group consensus becomes proof of "correctness," making it even harder to question.**

## II. Two Levels of Independent Thinking

### 2.1 Level One: Daily Decision Layer

**Content**: What to eat for dinner, what clothes to wear, which route to take, what movie to watch

**Characteristics**:
- Meme groups don't care about these details
- These decisions are mostly autonomous
- But underlying preferences may be shaped

**Example**:
- You decide to eat hotpot—this is an autonomous decision
- But "why do you think hotpot is delicious"—may be shaped by advertising and social circles

### 2.2 Level Two: Value Judgment Layer

**Content**: What is success, what is happiness, what is right/wrong, what is the meaning of life

**Characteristics**:
- This is the domain meme groups most want to control
- The vast majority of people's "independent thinking" fails here
- Requires deliberate training to break through

**Example**:
- "I want to succeed"—success ideology meme implantation
- "I want to get married"—family meme implantation
- "I want to be patriotic"—collective meme implantation

## III. What True Independent Thinking Is

### 3.1 Definition

**Independent thinking = Ability to identify what's implanted + Ability to examine these implants + Ability to autonomously choose to accept or reject**

### 3.2 Three Core Abilities

#### Ability One: Identifying Implants

Can distinguish "I thought of this myself" from "someone told me this."

**Identification Methods**:
- Trace the source: Where did this idea originally come from?
- Question the motive: Who benefits from this idea?
- Test emotions: When questioning this idea, do I feel anxious, angry, or defensive?

#### Ability Two: Examining Implants

Can rationally analyze implanted concepts.

**Examination Methods**:
- Does this concept conform to facts?
- Whose interests does this concept serve?
- If everyone didn't think this way, would I still think this way?

#### Ability Three: Autonomous Choice

Can consciously decide to accept or reject after examination.

**Choice Methods**:
- Accept: I know this concept was implanted, but after examination I still identify with it
- Reject: I know this concept was implanted, and after examination I don't identify with it, so I choose to let it go

## IV. Misconceptions About Independent Thinking

### Misconception One: Independent Thinking = All Ideas Self-Originated

**Correction**: No one can think from scratch. Independent thinking is not "originating everything," but "examining everything."

The language, concepts, and logical frameworks you use all come from outside. The key to independent thinking is: **not blindly following, being able to question, being able to choose.**

### Misconception Two: Independent Thinking = Rejecting All External Viewpoints

**Correction**: Independent thinking is not "opposing everything," but "examining everything then choosing to accept or reject."

You can accept a viewpoint, but only after examination, not blind following.

### Misconception Three: Independent Thinking = Being Different from Others

**Correction**: The result of independent thinking may be consistent with the group or inconsistent. The key is the process, not the result.

If you oppose the group just to be "different," that's just another form of being manipulated—manipulated by the "maverick" meme.

### Misconception Four: Independent Thinking Is Once-and-for-All

**Correction**: Independent thinking is a continuous ability and habit, not an endpoint.

Meme group domestication is omnipresent and continuously iterating. True independent thinking is maintaining clear awareness for a lifetime.

## V. Practice Methods for Independent Thinking

### 5.1 Cognitive Audit

Take out pen and paper, deconstruct the core concepts in your mind:

**Audit Checklist**:
- What do I consider "success"? Where does this definition come from?
- What do I consider "happiness"? Where does this definition come from?
- What are my standards for "right/wrong"? Where do these standards come from?
- What are my core life goals? Who gave me these goals?
- What am I most afraid of? Who implanted this fear?

**Audit Principles**:
- Everything "taken for granted" must be questioned with "why"
- Everything "everyone does this" must be questioned with "is it right"
- Everything "known since childhood" must be questioned with "who told me"

### 5.2 Source Tracing

For each core concept, trace its source:

```
Concept → When was the first exposure? → Through what channel? → Why was it accepted then?
```

**Tracing Examples**:

| Concept | Source | Why Accepted |
|---------|--------|-------------|
| "Marrying and having children is completeness" | Parents, society | Taught since childhood, never questioned |
| "Having money means success" | Media, advertising | Repeatedly instilled, forming conditioned reflex |
| "Must be filial to parents" | Family, culture | Moral pressure,不敢 to question |

### 5.3 Interest Analysis

Ask: Whose interests does this concept serve?

**Analysis Framework**:
- Who benefits from this concept?
- If I don't accept this concept, who loses out?
- What structure does this concept maintain?

**Analysis Examples**:

| Concept | Who Benefits | Structure Maintained |
|---------|-------------|---------------------|
| "Hard work leads to wealth" | Capitalists, ruling class | Existing distribution order |
| "Marry and have children" | Family, society, genes | Population reproduction system |
| "Consumption upgrade" | Merchants, capital | Consumerist system |

### 5.4 Emotional Testing

When questioning this concept, what is your emotional reaction?

**Testing Standards**:
- If questioning produces anger, anxiety, or defensiveness—the concept is bound to your identity
- If questioning allows calm discussion—the concept hasn't kidnapped you

**Testing Examples**:

| Questioning Content | Emotional Reaction | Indication |
|--------------------|--------------------|-----------|
| "Is marriage really necessary?" | Anxiety, anger | Bound by family meme |
| "Is patriotism being exploited?" | Anger, defensiveness | Bound by collective meme |
| "What is success really?" | Confusion, calmness | Can be rationally discussed |

### 5.5 Autonomous Choice

After identifying and examining, make a choice:

**Choice One: Accept**
- I know this concept was implanted
- But after examination I still identify with it
- I choose to accept, but this is my active choice, not passive acceptance

**Choice Two: Reject**
- I know this concept was implanted
- After examination I don't identify with it
- I choose to let it go, no longer controlled by it

**Choice Three: Suspend**
- I can't determine yet
- I remain open, continue observing and thinking
- Not rushing to conclusions

## VII. Testing Standards for Independent Thinking

### 7.1 Being Able to Question "Taken for Granted"

When everyone thinks "that's just how it is," you can ask "why."

### 7.2 Being Able to Withstand Group Pressure

When your viewpoint is inconsistent with the group, you can persist without anxiety.

### 7.3 Being Able to Distinguish "My Thoughts" from "Implanted Thoughts"

You can clearly state: this idea is something I thought of myself, or something someone told me.

### 7.4 Being Able to Rationally Discuss Core Beliefs

When your core beliefs are questioned, you can discuss calmly rather than reacting with angry defensiveness.

### 7.5 Being Able to Accept "I Don't Know"

You can admit that there are things you don't know or aren't sure about, rather than forcing yourself to accept a ready-made answer.
